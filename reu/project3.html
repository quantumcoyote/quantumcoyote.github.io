Synthetic Chabazite for Sequestration of Metal Ions Relevant in Nuclear Fission Waste Remediation

Dr. James Hoefelmeyer

Uranium is the second most abundant actinide on Earth and a major source of energy as a fissile material. As a result, 2000 tons of nuclear waste are produced in the US alone every year, while tens of thousands of tons more are produced worldwide. The sequestration of highly radioactive ions produced during nuclear fission is central to the development of an advanced nuclear energy cycle, waste treatment, and in developing remediation strategies for nuclear containment sites. Fission of uranium (233U or 235U) or plutonium (239Pu) can produce high yields of cesium (137Cs) and strontium (90Sr) radioactive isotopes with half-live of ~30 years. Due to the low ionization enthalpies of cesium and strontium, these ions are typically found as Cs+ and Sr2+ cations.

The sequestration and storage of these radioactive ions is a critical component of a safe nuclear fission program since both are highly environmentally hazardous. A number of high surface area materials can be used to bind cations, such as carbon, silica (or other metal oxides), metal sulfides, and zeolites. Zeolites are crystalline, porous materials, and can be found in a wide range of compositions and topologies. Their fundamental building block is a tetrahedral orthosilicate unit (SiO4) that has a chemical composition similar to quartz. However, the tetrahedral SiO4 groups in zeolites form 3D networks of channels and cages giving rise to their porosity in the crystalline solid. Substitution of an aluminum ion (Al3+) for a silicon ion (Si4+) in the framework results in sites with a charge deficiency in the framework that must be compensated with a proton (H+) to maintain electroneutrality. These Bronsted acid sites can be easily exchanged by cations, which can be used for the sequestration of metal ions such as Cs+ and Sr2+ relevant to nuclear fission waste remediation. The specific objective of this project is to prepare and characterize synthetic chabazite samples and investigate their capacity for the sequestration of Cs+ and Sr2+. In this project, we will utilize non-radioactive isotopes of the studied metals
