In the early 20th century, a series of scientific and technological advancements led to massive increases in worldwide agricultural production in what is known as the Green Revolution. One of the key elements of this revolution was the Haber-Bosch process that allowed for the industrial production of vast quantities of ammonia leading to the subsequent production of nitrogen fertilizers. However, not all of the nitrogen and phosphorous added to the soil makes it to the dinner table, thereby leading to environmental consequences such as the eutrophication of lakes and rivers. Eutrophication is the enrichment of an aquatic ecosystem with anthropomorphic nutrients, such as nitrates, directly washed from fertilizers or generated by bacteria. Ecosystems with an excess of nutrients suffer from a boom of microalgae and later hypoxia due to aerobic respiration and the decomposition of organic material endangering the ecosystems biodiversity. Furthermore, in the denitrification process, the reduction of nitrate to N2 occurs via the formation of nitric oxide. Nitric oxide is a free radical able to react with ozone depleting the ozone layer as well as evolving to nitrous oxide, a greenhouse gas 300 times more powerful than carbon dioxide in the presence of oxygen. Additionally, nitrogen oxides are also by-products of the combustion of fossil fuels.

My group studies the separation and catalytic decomposition of the key species in the dinitrification process.
